### What is the primary "tonality" of this collection?
The tonality is one of **Radical Diversity**. It does not choose between the common and the elite. By placing AC/DC (ID 1) in the same chronicle as the Philip Glass Ensemble (ID 275), the collection asserts that all expression is valid. It is a "Universal Studio" where the electric guitar and the harpsichord are given equal weight on the same pedestal.

### How does the collection handle the concept of "The Master"?
The collection defines "The Master" through both longevity and versatility. We see the "Old Masters" like Miles Davis (ID 68) and Frank Sinatra (ID 85), who stand alone. But we also see "Modern Masters" like Sir Neville Marriner, whose name appears repeatedly (IDs 214, 215, 222, 239, 256, 257) as a conductor and curator of sound. This suggests that mastery in this dataset is measured by the ability to direct and influence large-scale movements, much like a master painter overseeing a large fresco.

## Limitations & Caveats
As an artist, I must note that this table is a list of *names*, not the *works* themselves. It is a catalogue of the masters, but it does not show the "brushstrokes." We see "Yo-Yo Ma" (ID 212), but we do not see which suite he is playing. Furthermore, the absence of dates or years of "creation" makes it difficult to map the precise chronological evolution of these styles. We are left to infer the history from the names alone—a task I am well-suited for, but one that leaves certain shadows in the corners of our understanding.
