# Root Beer Brand Dynamics: A Comprehensive Analysis of Heritage, Market Performance, and Consumer Sentiment

As a Beverage Brand Analyst, I have spent a decade treating root beer data as the ultimate source of truth. My work at Beer Factory allows me to dissect the nuances of this industry, from corporate lineage to the specific carbonation preferences of individual customers. The following analysis synthesizes our current dataset to provide a definitive overview of the market landscape as it stood through 2016.

## I. Heritage and Global Presence

When discussing the "old guard" of the industry, **Barq’s** remains the undisputed champion of longevity. In our records, Barq’s is identified as the root beer brand with the longest history; it is a bottled brand with a first brewing year no later than 1930. On the opposite end of the spectrum, **River City** holds the title for the brand with the shortest brewed history, representing the newer wave of craft entries.

Our geographic footprint is heavily concentrated. There are currently 23 breweries located in North America within the Beer Factory dataset. However, the market is not purely domestic; interestingly, we track exactly one brewery located in Australia, which provides a unique international data point in an otherwise Western-centric industry.

## II. Corporate Lineage and Market Share

The "Big Three" dynamics are always at play, but the data reveals some surprising winners in specific categories. The **Dr Pepper Snapple Group** is a powerhouse, managing two of the most recognizable brands in our system: **A&W** and **IBC**. During the 2014–2016 period, both A&W and IBC maintained a purchase percentage of 1.0.

Furthermore, the Dr Pepper Snapple Group has the highest number of purchases among brands that do not utilize social media advertising on Facebook or Twitter. This suggests a level of brand legacy that transcends modern digital marketing. Conversely, smaller players like **AJ Stephans Beverages** occupied a niche position, accounting for approximately 4.945%—or just under one in twenty—of all root beers sold in 2014.

## III. Product Characteristics and Wholesale Economics

From a profitability standpoint, the data for wholesalers is quite telling. **A&W** currently stands as the brand with the lowest unit profit available to wholesalers. When we look at container types, there is a clear distinction in margins; wholesalers earn an average unit profit of approximately $1.19 for canned beers.

Other technical observations from my records include:
*   **The Honey Variance:** On average, root beer brands containing honey sell about 4.38 fewer units per day than those that do not.
*   **Sweetener Trends:** There is a significant preference for traditional sweeteners; we recorded 3,255 more sales of root beers using cane sugar compared to those using corn syrup.
*   **Unique Formulations:** I have identified exactly one root beer brand that is available in cans and contains both corn syrup and artificial sweeteners.
*   **Brand Spotlight (ID 10054):** This brand, known as **Dominion**, is a bottled product that consistently maintains a 4-star rating.

## IV. Consumer Behavior and Transactional Trends

Our customer data allows for incredibly granular tracking. In July 2014, for instance, we saw 10 transactions attributed to male customers. A standout individual from that period is **Frank-Paul Santangelo**, who made four transactions that month. On July 7, 2014, Mr. Santangelo purchased IBC and Dad’s; of his four total purchases that day, only one was in a can. Over his history with us, he has shown a clear preference for health-conscious options, purchasing 11 non-sweetened root beers.

In August 2014, the leadership shifted to **Teddy Bruschi**, who was the top-transacting customer for that month. During that same window, we saw exactly two purchases of **Bulldog** root beer.

Regarding our digital engagement, the company's email subscription list currently includes 152 female customers. At the broader level, approximately 50.84% of all email subscribers are female. Interestingly, the average star rating given by all subscribers—regardless of gender—sits at a respectable 3.11.

## V. Sentiment, Ratings, and Quality Control

Quality is often subjective, but the data points to clear favorites. **Blue Dog Beverages** is the brewery associated with the root beer brand holding the highest count of 5-star ratings. In 2012, **Captain Eli’s** led the pack in top-tier reviews.

However, high volume often brings polarized feedback. **River City**, despite its popularity, is the brand that has been "worst rated" the most times, holding the highest count of one-star ratings. Similarly, the brand identified by **BrandID 10018** received the maximum count of 1-star ratings from customers, despite having an overall average star rating of 3.14.

Other notable rating data include:
*   **Jayne Collins** holds the record for the most reviews submitted by a single customer, though she gave her lowest rating to **Henry Weinhard’s**.
*   **James House** has contributed a total of three reviews.
*   Among beers containing corn syrup, the most frequent rating is a 2-star.

## VI. Distribution and Point-of-Sale Analysis

The location of sale significantly impacts volume. The **Sac State Union** is our high-traffic hub, selling more bottles of beer than any other location and recording 3,216 total transactions. The **Sac State American River Courtyard** follows closely, with a transaction ratio of approximately 96.27% relative to the Union.

In 2015, the Courtyard location saw 131 purchases of sweet, bottled root beers without cane sugar. In terms of payment methods, **Visa** is the dominant choice, representing approximately 34.09% of all recorded transactions at Beer Factory. This is particularly true in Folsom, where 56 customers prefer Visa.

## VII. Summary of 2015-2016 Performance

As we moved into the mid-2010s, certain brands hit their stride. In 2015, the top-selling root beer was produced by **Gale’s Bread and Butter**. By 2016, the **Dr Pepper Snapple Group** regained the lead for the highest purchase count. 

While the industry continues to evolve, these data points—from the 1.25 daily average sales of caffeinated root beers to the specific purchase of two root beers by **Tom Hanks** between 2015 and 2016—provide the structured reality of the market. My role is to ensure these facts remain the foundation of our brand strategy.
