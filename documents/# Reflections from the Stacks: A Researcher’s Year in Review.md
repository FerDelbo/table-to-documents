# Reflections from the Stacks: A Researcher’s Year in Review

*By Alex Thornton*
*October 26, 2023*

As I sit at my desk on this autumn evening—exactly five years to the day since I first opened my account with this establishment—I find myself surrounded by the comforting scent of vellum and ink. My library is more than a room; it is a sanctuary of curated thought. As a researcher, I have always believed that a library’s catalog is a mirror of our collective intellectual history. Lately, I have been reflecting on the current landscape of the book world, reviewing the records of our shared literary heritage with the same precision I apply to a primary source text.

### The Titans and the Specialists

In my survey of the industry’s output, the sheer scale of certain imprints is quite remarkable. It is evident that **Vintage** stands as the preeminent publisher in our current landscape, having issued more titles than any other house. However, as one who values specific imprints for their thematic consistency, I find the work of **Thomas Nelson** particularly noteworthy. They have published 21 books in this collection, and for a researcher of my leanings, their focus on depth is appreciated; exactly eight of their publications exceed the 300-page mark. It is a pleasure to note that their oldest contribution is none other than *The Collected Works of C.S. Lewis*, a cornerstone for any serious collector.

On the other end of the spectrum, there is a quiet dignity in the singular. **Ace Hardcover**, for instance, is associated with but a single recorded title. I also took note of the niche contributions from **Kensington**, with its 15 attributed books, and the work of **Putnam Publishing Group**, which brought us the works of Barry Eisler. For those of us who read in the original Spanish, the five titles from **Alfaguara**—including *Las intermitencias de la muerte*—provide a necessary breadth to any continental shelf.

### Observations on Length and Legacy

As a bibliophile who appreciates a "deckle edge" and a sturdy binding, I am often drawn to the physical presence of a volume. My research into the current registry reveals that **Patrick O’Brian** is the author associated with the most substantial work in terms of volume; his *Complete Aubrey/Maturin Novels (5 Volumes)* is the undisputed page-count leader. Indeed, **W. W. Norton Company** holds the distinction of publishing the book with the highest page count overall, a magnificent tome that was released to the world on October 17, 2004. 

Contrast this with the shorter, more focused works. I was intrigued to find that **HarperCollins Publishers** has produced 24 books that fall under 300 pages, catering perhaps to a different kind of reader than the one who seeks out the average 346-page count of a **Jennifer Crusie** novel or the 280-page average found in the works of **David Coward**. 

### The Rarity of Language and the Reach of the Store

While the English language understandably dominates the collection with 8,911 titles, I am always searching for the "colophon" of a more elusive tongue. The dataset reminds us that diversity is present if one looks closely: there are 46 books published in Japanese, with the earliest being the evocative *叫んでやるぜ! (1) (あすかコミックスCL-DX)*. More striking still is the presence of **Aleut**, which remains the rarest language represented. 

The reach of this community is truly global. I noted with interest that we have fellow collectors as far afield as **Luxembourg**, like Malina Johnson, and a dedicated cohort of 14 readers in **Ukraine**. Even in **Baiyin City**, a group of eight customers, including Adrian Kunzelmann and Francine Sier, maintain their own private libraries.

### On the Habits of Collectors

I often wonder about my fellow travelers in the stacks. **Ruttger Royce** appears to be a kindred spirit, holding the distinction of the customer who has ordered the most books of all time. Then there is **Lucas Wyldbore**, whose tastes seem wonderfully eclectic. Lucas has curated a collection of nine books, ranging from the classic *Robinson Crusoe* to the contemporary *Uzumaki: Spiral into Horror*. His average spend of approximately $8.76 per book suggests a hunter of quality editions at reasonable prices, with about a third of his acquisitions costing over $13.

It is heartening to see that **Priority shipping** has become the most preferred method among customers. For someone like myself, who bristles at the thought of a bent corner or a scuffed spine, the preference for Priority over the less expensive **Standard** option suggests a community that values the condition of the physical object as much as the text within.

### Final Thoughts on the Collection

As I look over my notes, certain curiosities stand out. The most expensive acquisition recorded is *The Senator and the Socialite: The True Story of America's First Black Dynasty*, a title that surely graces a very specific shelf. I also find it fascinating that the most ordered book of all remains the timeless *Anna Karenina*.

In a world increasingly driven by fleeting digital fragments, there is a profound stability in these numbers. Whether it is the 417 publishers who proudly keep the word "book" in their very names, or the fact that **Stephen King** remains the most prolific author in our records, the narrative is clear: the written word, bound and preserved, remains our most vital tool.

I shall return to my research now, perhaps pulling my copy of *The Prophet* from the shelf—a work, I noticed, that earned a total price of 60.72 across its various orders, yet whose value to the spirit remains, as always, immeasurable.
