# Curating a Living Library: Observations from a Bibliophile’s Journey

As a graphic designer, my relationship with books has always been as much about the object itself as the prose within. Since I first joined this bookstore community back in June 2020, my shelves have transformed from a mere collection into a carefully curated gallery of design, history, and narrative. There is something deeply tactile about a hardcover edition—the weight of the paper, the choice of typeface, and the resilience of the binding—that a digital screen simply cannot replicate.

Lately, I’ve been reflecting on the state of my personal library and the broader world of publishing that feeds it. When you spend your days thinking about layout and visual hierarchy, you start to see patterns in the way books are produced and consumed.

## The Titans of the Shelf

It’s no surprise to me that Vintage ranks as the most prolific publisher in the current landscape; their distinctive trade paperbacks and high-quality editions seem to be everywhere. However, when I’m looking for something with a bit more historical gravity, I find myself looking at the catalog of Thomas Nelson. They’ve published 21 books in this particular ecosystem, and for a collector like myself, their "Collected Works of C.S. Lewis" remains a cornerstone—it's actually the oldest title they have listed. 

In terms of sheer physical presence, the "Complete Aubrey/Maturin Novels" by Patrick O'Brian is the undisputed heavyweight. Published by W. W. Norton Company, this five-volume set holds the record for the highest page count in the collection. As someone who appreciates a book that can act as a literal anchor for a room, it’s a masterpiece of publishing. I noted that the single book with the highest page count was released on October 17, 2004—a golden era for substantial, authoritative hardcovers.

## Design, Language, and the Niche

While the English-language collection is vast—totaling some 8,911 books—I’ve found myself increasingly drawn to international design. The Japanese-language segment, though smaller at 46 titles, offers some of the most avant-garde cover work I’ve seen. I was fascinated to learn that the earliest Japanese publication in our records is *叫んでやるぜ! (1)*. Exploring these niche corners is where I find the most inspiration for my own design work.

I’ve also been keeping an eye on specific author-publisher pairings that signify quality. For example, Abrams—a publisher I’ve always respected for their visual standards—is the home for authors like Audrey Niffenegger and Dan Nadel. On the other hand, if you’re looking for the classics, Penguin Classics remains the gold standard, particularly for their William Shakespeare editions, of which they’ve released two notable titles in this current set.

## The Pulse of the Reading Community

It’s interesting to see what my fellow readers are gravitating toward. While I tend to avoid the "bestseller" lists in favor of staff picks, there’s no denying the pull of a masterpiece like *Anna Karenina*, which stands as the top-ordered title overall. It’s heartening to see such a dense, complex work of fiction maintain that kind of popularity.

I’m also curious about how we all receive our books. I’ve always been a stickler for shipping; there is nothing more heartbreaking than a bent corner on a first edition. It seems I’m in good company, as Priority shipping is the most preferred method among customers. We clearly value speed and safety. 

Looking back at the data of our collective habits, 2020 was a landmark year—it recorded the highest number of customer orders, likely due to all of us seeking solace in pages during the lockdowns. Interestingly, that same year saw exactly 60 orders returned, a reminder that even in a peak year, the logistics of keeping a book in "mint" condition can be a challenge.

## Rare Finds and Collectibles

Every collector has a "white whale." In the world of high-value acquisitions, *The Senator and the Socialite: The True Story of America's First Black Dynasty* currently holds the title for the most expensive book in the catalog. While I usually stick to Art and Design History, a book of that significance and price point usually implies a certain level of production quality that piques my interest.

For those looking for historical significance, there are six books that stand out as having the greatest potential value as collectibles, including *Consider the Lilies* (the first book published in 1900) and several volumes of the *History of the Peloponnesian War*. These are the kinds of books that aren't just read; they are preserved.

## Final Reflections

Building a library is a slow, deliberate process. Whether it’s tracking down one of the 40 books by Orson Scott Card or seeking out a slim volume from HarperCollins (who, interestingly, have published 24 books under 300 pages), every addition tells a story. 

As I look at my own "to-read" list, I’m reminded of why I chose this bookstore four years ago. It’s about the community of people like Ruttger Royce—our most frequent orderer—and the specialized care that goes into distributing these works. From the 8,911 English titles to the rarest Aleut-language entries, the diversity of the written word continues to be my greatest source of inspiration.

Next on my list? I think it’s time I finally cracked the spine on a David Foster Wallace—apparently, there are five of his works to choose from here. I just hope the shipping is as careful as I’ve come to expect.
