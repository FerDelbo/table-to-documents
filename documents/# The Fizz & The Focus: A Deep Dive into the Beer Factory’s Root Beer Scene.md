# The Fizz & The Focus: A Deep Dive into the Beer Factory’s Root Beer Scene

*By Alex Miller | Senior Graphic Designer & Craft Enthusiast*

As a designer, I’m always looking at the details—the label art, the typography, and the "story" a brand tells before you even take a sip. But as a guy who spends his weekends hitting up taprooms and local festivals, what’s *inside* the bottle matters just as much as the aesthetic. Lately, I’ve been nerding out on the root beer selections over at the Beer Factory. 

We often talk about craft beer with such reverence, but a well-made, artisanal root beer deserves that same level of respect. I decided to dig into what’s happening in our local scene—from the ingredients and the history to the community of drinkers who keep these small-batch breweries going.

### The Old Guard vs. The New Wave

One thing I love about this hobby is the sense of history. If you’re looking for the "OG" of the group, look no further than **Barq’s**. It’s the brand with the longest brewed history in the dataset, with its first brewing year dating back no later than 1930. There’s something to be said for that kind of longevity. On the flip side, we have brands like **River City**, which has the shortest brewed history. It’s the "new kid on the block," and interestingly, it's a bit of a polarizer. While about 28% of its ratings are a perfect five stars, it also holds the dubious honor of being the brand that’s been worst-rated the most times. That’s the craft life, right? You either love the bold experimentation or you don't.

Speaking of the landscape, we’ve got a solid **23 breweries across North America** represented here. It’s a healthy scene, though I noticed a lone standout brewery from Australia making the list—shout out to the Bundaberg fans (I see you, Peg Winchester).

### Ingredients Matter: The Sugar Divide

If you’ve followed my blog for a while, you know I’m a "Quality over Quantity" guy. I’ll take one bottle of something complex over a six-pack of corn-syrup-water any day. The data really backs up this preference in our community. In our local transactions, there were **3,255 more cane sugar root beers sold than those using corn syrup**. That’s a massive gap and a clear signal that we value real ingredients. 

Interestingly, while most of us are chasing that cane sugar high, there's a specific niche for non-sweetened options. I noticed **Frank-Paul Santangelo**, a regular at the shop, has picked up 11 non-sweetened root beers. I also spotted that brands containing honey actually sell about 4.38 fewer units per day than those that don't. Maybe the honey profile is just a bit too niche for the daily drinker?

And for my fellow label-readers: if you’re looking for those rare, bottled gems with a higher price point (over $2), the average price settles right at $3.00. Worth it for the craftsmanship.

### The Community "Third Place": Sac State & Beyond

A huge part of craft culture is where you drink. The **Sac State Union** and the **Sac State American River Courtyard** seem to be the two main hubs. The Union is the king of the bottle, selling more bottled root beer than any other location (3,216 total transactions). It’s also the place to go if you’re looking for **Dog n Suds**; they lead the Courtyard in those sales.

The Courtyard isn't slouching, though—it accounts for nearly half (about 49.05%) of all sales. In 2015 alone, they moved 131 sweet, bottled root beers that were specifically cane-sugar-free but non-artificial. It’s a specific vibe over there.

### The People Behind the Reviews

I’ve got to give a shout-out to the regulars. **Jayne Collins** is basically our local legend, holding the title for the most reviews submitted. She’s tough, though—she gave her lowest rating to Henry Weinhard’s (sorry, Henry). Then there’s **James House**, who’s contributed three solid reviews, and **Teddy Bruschi**, who was on a tear in August 2014 with the most transactions of the month.

I also love seeing the specific "hauls." On July 7, 2014, Frank-Paul Santangelo (our non-sweetened fan) went on a bit of a run, grabbing IBC and Dad’s. Interestingly, of the four he bought that day, only one was in a can. That tracks—canned root beer only made up about 16% of the 2014 purchases. We definitely prefer the clink of glass.

### Final Thoughts on the "Wall of Beer"

When I walk into a place like the Beer Factory, I want to know what I’m getting. The brands that consistently hit that five-star threshold—**IBC, River City, Frostie, Sprecher, Thomas Kemper, and Virgil’s**—are the ones I’m usually recommending to friends. 

The data tells a story of a community that’s local-heavy (with 1,192 transactions for California-based brands), ingredient-conscious, and loyal. Whether you’re like **Tom Hanks** (who apparently only bought two root beers between 2015 and 2016—come on, Tom, live a little!) or a "whale" like **Max Baer** or **Jim Breech**, there’s a seat at the table.

Just a heads up for the wholesalers out there: **A&W** might have the lowest unit profit for you, but it’s still moving, even if it had a weirdly quiet August back in 2014. 

Keep it local, keep it craft, and I'll see you at the next release.
